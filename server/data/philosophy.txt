Philosophy begins with the question of how we should live our life.
The ancient Stoics taught that virtue is the only true good and that fortune is indifferent.
Socrates believed an unexamined life is not worth living.
To trust in reason and seek the truth requires courage and an open mind.
Aristotle argued that happiness comes from living according to our nature and exercising virtue.
The conscience is that inner voice that tells us right from wrong.
Existentialists like Sartre claimed we are condemned to be free and must bear the weight of choice.
Every action we take matters because it defines who we are.
Nietzsche declared that the heart of philosophy is the will to power and self-overcoming.
The Epicureans sought peace of mind through simple pleasures and the absence of pain.
Eastern philosophy teaches that suffering arises from attachment and desire.
Buddhist meditation trains the mind to observe thoughts without judgment.
The question of death has driven philosophy since its beginning.
Plato believed the soul is immortal and that death is merely a transition.
Words and language shape how we think about the world and our place in it.
The search for meaning continues as long as conscious beings ask why they exist.
